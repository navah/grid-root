welcome to navah dot site :)

My name is Navah (NA-vah, rhymes with 'lava'). This website is a showcase (or storage space) for projects and activities. I am particularly interested in numerical modeling and visualizing quantitative information.

Right now I'm working in Chicago SaaS, doing data science, automation, and a bit of frontend. A few of my projects are mentorship opportunities for others on my team who are eager to build their tech chops!

My PhD research in computational geophysics (University of Chicago, 2020) applied tried-and-true numerical methods to new questions in climate physics and chemistry. Other scientific exploits include fluid dynamics in Earth's mantle and evolutionary biology.

If you came here looking for JokesByDad, Ted Cruz fanfiction, Ben Bernenke fanfiction, or Climate Shawty, I'm sorry to report that those projects have been retired. However, I am still the climate shawty and am happy to help with any climate science questions.

climate science

Carbon moves between the Earth's atmosphere, ocean, and crust. This is the inorganic carbon cycle (pictured here), and it operates over millions of years. Other carbon fluxes happen much more quickly, such as carbon uptake by plants and burning of fossil fuels.

Carbon dioxide (CO2) gas is well known because it impacts the Earth's climate and habitability. All processes that move carbon around can therefore affect the climate! The downward arrow labeled 'seafloor weathering' shows carbon moving from atmosphere to ocean to crust. That has been a research focus of mine.

numerical modeling

Numerical modeling is a great way to study systems too big or too slow or too inaccessible to observe directly. But deciding the model's scope and detail is an art AND a science.

In the second figure, I show a case where I decided it was appropriate to add detail where others have not. In short, reactive fluid travels through a porous medium, and my model added the ability for that fluid to meander into tiny nooks and crannies in the pore space.

modeling nitty gritty

Numerical modeling needs some basis in reality. Using physical/chemical observations in models is a good start. But how to make sure those observations are translated to the computer without losing meaning along the way?

Sometimes we can't, and we have to make corrections afterwards. In many cases, we can predict when and where meaning will be lost so we know how to correct for that. That's what is happening in the third figure.
